there is a house that was never finished
before someone came knocking.

the foundation still wet,
the beams still arguing with the wind,
and yet a guest arrived early,
boots already untied,
asking to be let in
before the floor had cured.

maybe that was the first mistake,
not love, but the calendar.
a door opened before its hinge had set,
two unfinished rooms
mistaken for a finished home.

call it a wrong-season planting.
a seed put in soil
that hadn't decided yet
whether it wanted to grow alone
or grow at all.

---

there was a time the builder gave away
every plank meant for his own roof,
believing a roof matters less
than the warmth of someone standing under it.
gave the good wood,
kept the splinters for himself,
and called it devotion
because no one had taught him
that some hands take wood
and never learn to build anything back.

he carried thorns thinking they were petals,
told himself the bleeding
was just how flowers feel
when they're finally being held.

---

the guest, once, held an old wound out like a gift,
something kept folded since childhood,
something that took years to even name out loud,
and was met not with stillness,
but with hunger,
a hand already reaching for the next page
before the first sentence had finished trembling.

the guest, before this house had any walls,
used to laugh at the builder's frame,
the wrong kind of broad,
the wrong kind of soft,
sent him pictures of prettier houses
and said, look, isn't this one lovely,
not knowing, or maybe knowing,
that comparison is a quiet kind of arson.

and even after moving in,
a name from a screen
still wandered the guest's sleep
like a tenant who never paid rent
but never left either.

---

the guest said, once, plainly,
this house was built without a hearth.
no fire here. never was. never will be.
asked the builder to be warm enough
for two.

and the builder tried.
god, he tried.
kept both flames burning
on a single matchstick
until his fingers blistered
and the guest never asked why
they smelled like smoke.

---

slowly, the builder stopped handing over planks for free.
started counting.
started asking what return a window might bring
before installing another one.
the guest noticed.
the guest, for the first time, panicked
at the thought of an empty doorway,
begged the builder to stay inside it,
and something in the begging was real,
the kind of real that arrives
only after the cost has already been paid.

so the house changed.
slowly.
the guest tried to sweep a little,
mend a hinge or two,
stopped laughing at the wrong-shaped beams.

but old roots are slow learners.

a trip got promised to a stranger
in the same week
a promise had been made to stay,
and the builder watched the guest pack a bag
lighter than any apology ever offered,
and understood,
quietly,
that priority is not what people say.
it's what they pack first.

---

there was an orchard the builder planted by hand,
seeds meant to fix what biology refused to fix on its own,
months spent convincing soil
that it deserved better fruit,
the solution
a few footsteps away
turned into a mountain
that took a season to climb together.

and still,
the meals split unevenly,
where one plate
keeps refilling itself
from the other's portion,
and never once asks if there's enough left.

---

something happens to gentle hands
that hold a hammer too long without rest.
they begin, against their own will,
to remember how to swing.

the builder used to write soft things.
now he flinches before a doorway opens.
now ordinary footsteps in the hall
sound like old storms returning.
now a kind word from the guest
gets checked twice for hidden weather
before it's allowed inside.

this is the quiet wreckage no one warned him about,
that the softest rooms in a person
can be renovated into something harder
simply by living too long
beside someone who never closes a door gently.

and when the storms came,
the guest would say, enough now, let's become sunny again,
the way someone might offer a flower
to a room that's still on fire,
sweet, useless, and somehow making it worse.

---

coins kept dropping into a well
that never sent an echo back.
for orchard's growth,
things bought with wages earned
in someone else's spare hours.
and the well stayed quiet,
not cruel exactly,
just never curious enough
to ask where the coins came from.

a love that used to pour like rivers
without checking the tide,
learned, eventually, to measure itself in cups.
and a river that starts measuring
has already stopped being a river.

---

two rooms in this house
maybe weren't meant to share a wall.

one room arrived already a child,
told from birth to hold up
ceilings far too heavy for small hands,
and somewhere along the way,
a guest leaned a little too long
on a builder who arrived too early in its story,
and a sapling that should have learned
to stand without a trellis
never got the chance to try.

maybe, in another version of this map,
the roads don't cross here at all.
maybe the guest grows fully
in some other weather,
becomes whoever it was always becoming,
without a builder there to catch every fall
before the falling teaches anything.

both rooms know this now.
both hate the architecture a little,
the timing more than the people in it,
and the strange cruelty of being right
for someone
at entirely the wrong hour.

---

and then there was the night
a different house almost burned.
midnight, no warning,
flames close enough to swallow
a whole life's work,

a line outside stayed dead for months,
current denied by a hand
asking for more than was owed,
a debt taken just to keep light legal
in a summer the planet itself
couldn't survive without record.

a friend, an ordinary one,
called once, just to ask
if the smoke had cleared.

the guest, though,
had a new house now,
somewhere with garlands and lights.

and a builder who has rebuilt
half a stranger's foundation
out of love
wonders, quietly, what he ever was
to someone who never once asked
if his own roof was still there.

---

attachment, some old book says,
is the root of every ache.
a wave gets attached to its shore
and forgets it was born from the whole ocean.
a root grows so deep into another's foundation
that pulling it free
would crack both houses at once.

and maybe that's the entire story.
no villain, or victim,
just two unfinished foundations
that grew into each other
before either one had cured properly,
now too tangled to separate
without taking the other down too.

---

still.

even now,
with the walls unpainted,
the windows still arguing with the wind,
a name still wandering some unfinished sleep,
and silence answering most knocks
where two voices used to live,

the door has not been nailed shut.

a broken flower vase resting in the corner,
books scattered across the bed like forgotten prayers,
and sharp creases in the sheets holding every memory,
a lantern stays lit near the threshold,
out of habit, maybe,
or out of something more stubborn than habit,
the kind of light a person leaves on
not because they expect anyone home soon,
but because putting it out completely
would mean admitting
the house never mattered.

it mattered.
it still does.
ruined floors and all.

so if, by mistake or memory,
footsteps ever return to this doorway again,
the route of which only one person knows,
do not be startled
when you see yourself reflected,
in these unfinished walls.
no, do not be startled.
after no matter what, 
no one can paint them again.

so when the sun sets,
come here too.
do not lose yourself in the distances.
at least once in a while,
even by mistake or memory,
come and fill this house of mine with your fragrance.
at least once in a while,
even by mistake,
let this ruined home remember
what it felt like
to be warm again.

the same fool would still be found here,
who never learned, not fully,
how to stop loving a house
just because it forgot, for a while,
how to be one.
